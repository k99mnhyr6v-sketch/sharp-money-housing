B
Comp De-Rating: Adjusting Neighborhood Comps for Size and the Rate Environment They Sold In
A smaller comp that sold at 3% tells you even less than it looks like — two adjustments, applied separately

Use this when the subject property last sold long ago (so Exhibit B doesn't apply directly) but the seller/agent is anchoring price off neighborhood comps from 2020–2023. Each comp gets adjusted in two separate steps so you can see how much of the gap is size and how much is rate: (1) normalize to the subject's square footage via $/sqft, (2) re-price that size-normalized figure at today's rate, holding monthly payment constant. Add a row per comp.

Limitation, stated plainly: $/sqft is a blunt normalization. It doesn't capture lot size, finish quality, layout, or condition — two houses can be the same square footage and not be comparable. Use this to size-correct the obvious mismatch, not as a precise appraisal substitute. If a comp's size is within ~10% of the subject, the size adjustment will be small and the rate adjustment is doing most of the work; if it's a real mismatch (1,800 vs 2,800 sqft), the size step matters a lot and is worth a sentence of explanation if you cite it.

Embedded Wear & Deferred Capital
Remaining-useful-life model — not a tax depreciation claim, a physical one

Defaults below are typical industry lifespans and 2026 mid-Atlantic per-sqft rates for size-dependent components (roof, flooring, siding); HVAC/water heater/appliances are flat estimates since they don't scale much with house size. Edit any row with actual age, condition, or quoted replacement cost as you learn it — this is meant to be corrected per property, not trusted blind.

Assumption: every component below is assumed original to construction (installed at year 0) unless you override its age individually. Most seller disclosures are sparse and won't confirm this — if a disclosure mentions a specific replacement ("new roof 2019"), use the per-row age override below instead of relying on house age alone.
Inferential, not confirmed: this adjusts flooring's effective age upward (~40%) to reflect that pet households tend to show faster functional wear on floors than chronological age suggests — claw marks on hard surfaces, stains and odor in carpet padding or subfloor. Scoped to flooring only; exterior siding wasn't included because pet-related wear there is too localized (door scratching, paw prints) to fairly scale across an entire siding replacement cost. This is a reason to inspect closely, not a fact to cite in a negotiation unless confirmed by disclosure or your own inspection.
